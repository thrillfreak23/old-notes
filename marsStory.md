It was raining on Mars when Idimon Pentoi disembarked.  The Sun was
father away than he was used to back home on Yrth, and in low gravity,
clouds grow thicker and darker, so he emerged from the *Hypnotic
Chamber of Illar* into a downpour in dim gray twilight.  An enormous
bolt of lightning cut the world in half then disappeared.  He'd heard
thunder before but it was rare in the vast salt deserts of Yrth, which
were silent and sterile compared to this noise and stink and ...
uncleanliness.  

Idimon Pentoi was fine with uncleanliness.  Despite their reputation
for a fearsome asceticism, the monastics of the salt plains were for
the most part not judgemental people.  To judge everything according
to its nature and not according to your own was one of the highest
commandments.  *Purity is a ghostlight, follow it not.  Perfection is
delusion, do not seek it.  The Mundum is a mosaic, celebrate all the
broken pieces.*  He felt the words taught him by his religion were not
only beautiful but also outstandingly useful and true.  

He planned to discuss these things with Yokanân, his native
acquaintance, fellow scholar, and priest of the Holy Martian Catholic
Church.  Asynchronous electronic epistles were good and useful, but
talking with his friend face to face for the first time was a thing
he'd keenly anticipated for years.  

--*Keen, sharp, acute:* these are the words that came to people when
they met Pentoi and it wasn't his face alone that made them feel this
way, even though his craggy features did suggest it.  It was his
powers of observation, his mind, and his utter and disarming lack of
guile that made people see his acuity.  Lack of guile did not make him
simple, it was the grounds and necessary condition of his sharpness.
It was a whetstone to his steel, because without guile he was free,
unconfined and undimmed by a myriad phantasms that complicated and
clouded the thoughts of most homin.  This too he attributed to his
religion.  

He got into a cab pulled by elongate Martian animals called *horses*.
Like most living things here, horses were Yrthish in origin, but they
could only be found on Mars now.  Compared to the fossils of their
ancestors, these Martian animals were slimmer and more gracile, much
as the Martian people were compared to Pentoi.  Most were taller than
he was, and he knew he'd never see one as broad across in the chest.
To most sapients, those of Venus for example, the Martian air was
thin.  To the Yrthishman, though, it was thick.  

*"Î yuren kest."  --It's five standards.*  Pentoi's purse hung from
his belt.  He opened the mouth of it and selected five tiny osmiridium
coins, handed them up to the driver and replied formally in the local
Jagarôth tongue because it was as yet the only way he knew how to:
*"Vargaxâlen tepsôdh." I am obliged to you.*

He thought he'd never seen anything so exotic as the neon sign
blindingly bright above the front door of his hostel.  *CHEAP ROOMS*
it said, in bright pink light, in Galactic.  Just then he felt a hand
at his belt.  At his purse.  He acted, much faster than the lightning
had been.  This too stemmed from his religious training.  The old man
whose hand it was shrieked like a little child.  In the stare-bright
light he howled gibberish at his broken thumb and index finger,
drooling, and attempted to get away.  But Pentoi had him by the
collar, even though he had to reach up to grab it.

"Be careful, friend.  I'd hate to see you in greater pain."  He let
the elder go, and that individual disappeared across the street and
into an alleyway.  Pentoi only watched him vanish with a quizzical
smile and then he entered the hostel.

Runnerbats had taken an infant in Jarthax.  They had opened the front
door of a house on the edge of town in the middle of the night,
stalked in, and took only the one child.  The red dirt street was
tracked with the spoor of them.  Yokanân set his eyeglasses down on
his desk before him where he sat, pinched the bridge of his nose with
thumb and forefinger and sighed.  

He made the Sign of the Cross, then looked for a moment to be in
silent prayer.  "Has the child been found, or the child's body, by
some miracle perhaps?"

"No, Father.  But you know that," the Reeve told him gently.  One
would think the man had a habit of taking care of the good Father,
which was in fact the truth.

"Yes I suppose I do," he told the Reeve.  Father Yokanân had a harried
look, as though his world were a little more than he could manage.  It
*was*, he knew, more than he could manage, but such is true of every
sapient the whole Galaxy round.  He told himself this quite often.  He
had a quick mental image of the hideous mammals sprinting into the
darkness of the deep desert on the limbs that used to be their
ancestors' wings, one of them holding a bloodied screaming baby in the
prehensile hands that dangled beneath its body, shrill shrieks and
clicks of echolocation fading into the outer dark.

Jarthax was a small town on the outskirts of the Low-Canals Region.
Yokanân was no bishop. He was only a priest in the city of Trellin,
where there was a church every other block. He had no authority in the
case concerning the predation of the baby. What he did have was
experience of the Xeric Wastelands to which the *gîgôken* had
seemingly returned, and the oases hidden in that desert, one of which
would be the runnerbats' dwellingplace.  In most respects he was not a
hard man, but his father had been a hunter and taught him to hunt. He
might not put a stop to the killings entirely, but he could prevent
some deaths, of that much he was certain. God knew no one else
probably would even try.
