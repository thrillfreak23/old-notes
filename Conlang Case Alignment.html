ChatGPT said:

You’ve basically run into a classic alignment problem: one verb is semantically doing two jobs—a transfer (“give”) and a comparison (“outdo”)—and each of those wants to “own” an argument.

Natural languages solve this in a few different ways. You don’t actually need a third “object case” if you rethink how the comparison is encoded.

1. Treat the comparison as an oblique (most common fix)

Instead of making “Tam” a second object, mark it with a dedicated comparative marker—functionally like a preposition (“than”).

Kak outgave money than Tam to the church.

So structurally:

  • money → accusative (true direct object)

  • church → dative (recipient)

  • Tam → comparative/oblique case or particle

Many languages do exactly this. The “standard of comparison” is not treated as an object but as an adjunct.

You could invent:

  • a comparative case (like “-than”)

  • or a particle/postposition (“tam-THAN”)

This keeps your case system clean.

2. Make the comparison subordinate (very naturalistic)

Instead of cramming everything into one clause:

Kak gave more money to the church than Tam (did).

Now “Tam” belongs to a separate, elided clause:

  • Kak gave money to church

  • Tam gave (less) money to church

This avoids the triple-object problem entirely. Many real languages strongly prefer this strategy.

3. Promote the comparison to primary, demote the giving

If your prefixes (“out-”, “under-”) are central, flip the structure:

Kak exceeded Tam in giving money to the church

Now:

  • Tam → accusative (object of “exceed”)

  • “giving money to the church” → some kind of nominalized clause

This is elegant if your conlang likes verbal nouns or participles.

4. Use a special applicative / voice system

If you want something more “conlang-y” and systematic:

You could allow only one core object slot, and others get marked via:

  • applicatives

  • oblique cases

  • or voice alternations

So depending on what you want to foreground:

  • Theme voice → money is core

  • Recipient voice → church is core

  • Comparative voice → Tam is core

This is more complex but very powerful.

5. Rank your arguments (a simple rule-based solution)

You can also just define a hierarchy:

Direct object > recipient > comparison standard

Then:

  • money → accusative

  • church → dative

  • Tam → forced into oblique/comparative marking

What I’d recommend

Unless you’re aiming for something exotic, the cleanest solution is:

👉 Give “Tam” a dedicated comparative marker (case or particle).

It mirrors how many natural languages handle “than,” avoids overloading your case system, and keeps your verbal prefix idea intact.

  • church → dative

  • Tam → comparative oblique

Focus on beating Tam:

Kak OUT-COMP-gave Tam in-giving money to church

  • You never need more than one accusative

  • You get meaningful alternations, not just bookkeeping

  • Speakers can choose what’s important:

    • the money?

    • the recipient?

    • the rival?

That’s exactly how many real languages behave with applicatives and voice systems.

Design choices you can make

You’ve got a lot of freedom here:

How many voices/applicatives?

You could have:

  • recipient applicative (“give to X” → X becomes object)

  • comparative applicative (“outdo X” → X becomes object)

What happens to demoted arguments?

Common options:

  • oblique case

  • preposition/postposition

  • nominalization (“in the act of giving…”)

How visible is the morphology?

  • prefixes (fits your current system nicely)

  • suffixes

  • particles

A small warning (useful, not prohibitive)

If you go this route, commit to it across the language, not just for this verb.

It’ll feel much more natural if:

  • “give,” “show,” “tell,” etc. all behave similarly

  • “out-” works consistently with other verbs too

Bottom line

Option 4 isn’t just a fix—it’s a design philosophy:

“Only one object at a time. If you want a different one, promote it.”

That tends to produce languages that feel structured and expressive rather than overloaded.

Core rule (the elegant solution)

1. Prefixes form a hierarchy (outer > inner)

Verbal prefixes are not independent operators. They are ordered layers of grammatical prominence.

So:

OUT- DAT- V

is not “two operations acting equally,” but:

OUT- (dominant frame) wrapping DAT- (subordinate frame) wrapping the verb

2. Only the outermost prefix controls the Accusative

  • The outermost prefix determines what becomes the core object

  • Everything else is forced into oblique or non-core status

3. Inner prefixes become semantic, not structural

They still matter—but only in meaning, not syntax.

What this fixes immediately

No more “three objects competing.”

Instead:

  • one syntactic winner (outer layer)

  • one semantic structure underneath

  • everything else demoted predictably
