## Geography and Number of speakers

Bordering Syrtis Major to the east and stretching west to the oasis
called Thermopyle, the precincts of which border in turn the city
states Hekate and Nyx, which are forbidden to Terrans, the Low-Canals
region of Mars is vast, varied, and some say venomous.  Ninety-two
percent of the population is enslaved to the remainder, and this
supermajority labors in the fields of the latifundia or in the fumes
of the manufactoria; farming and industry alike thrive in the
Low-Canals.  According to Martian mores and their baroque and
byzantine legal systems (note the plural) they are slaves, but they
are not unrecompensed.  At night the windows of each house along the
narrow streets of the slave precincts glow warmly; hunger is rare, the
people are well-fed.  Drugs and alcohol enjoyed openly, the
omnipresence of the Holy Martian Catholic Church in every
neighborhood, subspace radio dramas rebroadcast on the vox and
discussed endlessly, cheap sex available in the thousands of brothels
along the canal fronts, crime, an ages long obsession with dueling:
these factors and more have been cited as the true chains of the
Low-Canals' slave population.  The vector of passivity that has most
enfeebled the slave class is, however, in this writer's opinion,
addiction to the Reticulum, which is nothing more than a network of
computers, the use of which is universal, more catholic in fact than
their Catholic Church, strange as such an addiction seems to sane
sapients.

Yet there is a lurid draw to the place, in this writer's estimation,
that attracts the outcast, the outlaw, the down and out.
Paradoxically the Low-Canals is a refuge of freedom for those
offworlders that come to visit, or even dwell here.  A law-bound land
of slaves where I am more free than I ever was on my homeworld Skâr.
My homilies resound in perfect Jagarôth in the fragrant smoky nave of
my nearly empty church.  My faith has faltered, but I am free.
